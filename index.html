The MoMA Pipeline

From creation to showcase at the New York's Museum of Modern Art
How do artworks actually make their way into MoMA's collection? We mapped the trajectories of 300 contemporary artists who entered MoMA's Painting & Sculpture collection between 2000 and 2025. This investigation traces that ecosystem — outlining the clusters of galleries and institutions where artists build capital, identifying the key decision-makers who transition them between worlds, and charting the five distinct routes — both transparent and opaque — that determine entry into the museum.

Overview · New York Circuit

The validation ecosystem before MoMA

56% of MoMA's Painting & Sculpture debuts between 2000 and 2025 arrived via private donation — not curatorial purchase. The curatorial voice peaked in 2018 at 82%, then collapsed to 29% after 2019. Behind these numbers lies a precise ecosystem: 36 key decision-makers and 5 distinct geographic clusters of galleries and institutions in New York where artists build the institutional capital that precedes museum entry.

This prior validation is a near-mandatory prerequisite: 87.6% of the analyzed artists had been featured in at least one recognized institution before their MoMA debut.
Route A · Chelsea Circuit

Chelsea Circuit

The Route A epicentre. Ann Temkin's curatorial eye works this circuit. Hauser & Wirth, Greene Naftali, David Zwirner, and Gagosian each have documented pipelines to MoMA. The Buchholz-to-MoMA pipeline (German contemporary) and the Zwirner-to-MoMA pipeline are the most consistent routes identified.

The Decision · Collectors & Trustees

Who Actually Decides

Collectors have played the most significant role in any artist's access to MoMA's collection — finding, donating, or selling them to the museum. 56% of MoMA's Painting & Sculpture debuts between 2000 and 2025 arrived via private donation. In contrast, curatorial voice peaked in 2018 at 82%, then dropped to 29% after 2019.

Among external collectors—those without a formal seat on the board— Patricia Phelps de Cisneros is the most significant case. Based in Caracas, Madrid, and New York, she spent decades building one of the world's largest collections of Latin American art through the Fundación Cisneros. The nine artists she introduced to MoMA—including Cildo Meireles, Beatriz Milhazes, and Gabriel Orozco—came directly from the Brazilian, Venezuelan, and Mexican art scenes, bypassing Chelsea galleries and American biennials. Without her, there would have been no Latin American pipeline into the Painting and Sculpture department during that period. Jean Pigozzi is the second external collector with a measurable impact. A Geneva-based businessman and photographer, he built the CAAC (Collection d'Art Africain Contemporain) over twenty years alongside curator André Magnin, who had discovered the artists via the 1989 “Magiciens de la Terre” exhibition in Paris. In 2019, he donated 45 works to MoMA in a single batch—the largest individual donation of the period—introducing Bodys Isek Kingelez, Romuald Hazoumè, and Moké into the collection. None of these artists had previously been represented in American institutions prior to the donation. Agnes Gund, the museum’s president emerita for 11 years, is associated with 23 acquisitions during this period—the highest individual volume identified. Ann Temkin, Chief Curator of Painting and Sculpture, controls all institutional purchasing decisions and is linked to 23 acquisitions made via purchase funds. Marie-Josée Kravis, Chair of the Board of Trustees, is associated with 13 acquisitions. Some trustees identified in the research also operate as collectors within the very field where they cast votes are, this is the case of Agnes Gund, Marie-Josée Kravis, Pamela Joyner, and the Ehrenkranz family.
